How to handle LLMs carefully

Because of the resources LLMs consume, we have an ethical duty to consider how we use them.

A means not an end

Conversations with LLMs are costly but may be valuable, the means to cover ground previously inaccessible, and to explore ideas otherwise beyond an individual or team's reach.

An LLM can be:

  • A tool for creating other tools
  • A means of iterating over a problem or idea
  • A way to explore (and not a crutch)

Since the interface is natural language, the conversation is, however, two way; LLMs listen as well as speak. Therefore, as we ponder their performance, we must consider our own.

Adapting not adopting

This piece is specifically about how web development teams might adapt to LLMs. It would be rash to incorporate them into development processes. Given their unsuitability for many tasks, and the cost of running them, we advise against wrapping them around existing practices or embedding them too deeply. Use them instead as we might conversations, as a way to explore, discover new approaches, and move on. They are useful but disposable though the artefacts we create as a result may persist.

Web development today

  • The cost of web app and page development is high
  • Interfaces must adapt to a variety of devices and visitors
  • LLMs are widely used by web development teams
  • LLMs are perceived of as assisting people, but may replace them
  • LLMs are used to increase productivity and to educate

Jakob Nielsen has suggested that generative AI could lead to Individualized UI.

Development teams could use LLMs (generative AI) to create multiple custom UIs.

Alternatively, an LLM running externally could help a user create an interface specific to their needs.

Should development teams carry on as though LLMs are a fad, or plan for fundamental change?

The value of APIs

LLMs need data to act upon, and data must be secured.

LLMs cannot be be given unfettered access to databases or have knowledge of their internal schema.

APIs provide a controlled and safe way access to data whilst preserving company privacy and honouring data protection issues.

But APIs might form the bridge between what teams do now and what might be expected of them.

And the role of the API can be extended. A company contract or constitution that expands on an API schema would become the single source of truth:

  • From which the API is derived
  • From which the database scheme is derived
  • Which front and backend developers use as the contract in contract-first development whilst iterating on the API
  • As the reference point for front and backend tests
  • Be the repository for tariffs and pricing details
  • Be the repository for business rules and statements pertaining to ethics, sustainability and accessibility

Writing and maintaining a schema is hard. It may be possible for internal and external LLMs to rely only on API endpoints and examples.

How should teams proceed?

Existing teams

  • Create an API if there isn't one
  • Internal benefits
    • Single source of truth in contract-first development
    • Single source of truth for front and backend tests
  • A starting point for 'competing' interfaces including those created by LLMs

New teams

New teams can adopt new structures and roles.

  • Write company contract which is the single source of truth
  • Create new structures and roles based on new requirements

Creating highly expressive company contracts will not be trivial.

Downsides

  • APIs 'hijacked' by LLMs interacting directly with APIs on behalf of end users (other companies, individuals)
  • Maintenance of the contract
  • Loss of detail
  • Black box/es
  • Loss of jobs
  • Loss of knowledge and broader understanding
  • Generic approaches
  • Loss of 'new' ideas and approaches; evolutionary stagnation

Upsides

  • Teams prepare for inevitable change (even though nature of change uncertain)
  • They retain knowledge by adaptation creating teams that are better suited to ecosystems in which LLMs are prevalent.
