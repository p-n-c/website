What makes a suitable site

Reducing unnecessary interactions is the starting point for improved performance, accessibility, security and sustainability (PASS).

It's a no show

The first step to improving a site's PASS, is to whittle it down.

  • Only send what is necessary
  • Only send what is necessary for the visitor
  • Only send what can be used on the visitor's device

Who needs what when

When we write, we have an audience in mind. This is also true of building apps. This article, for example, is aimed at SMEs with reasonable budgets and experienced teams who want to improve their app's PASS. Who is the app's audience?

Finding your audience

Or audiences. On a site such as a newspaper that is broadcasting to its readership, there may be little variability in the content. An app, which is receiving as well as disseminating data, may have multiple states, and many ways of engaging with it with, for different reasons.

Let's imagine how employees at an intergovernmental site monitoring carbon emissions might interact with their data.

  • Big picture: charts, executive summaries, key stats, takeaways
  • Analysis: interactive charts, models, scenarios, generating reports and new requirements, summarising data, picking out key data points and writing takeaways
  • Data entry: inputting data, overseeing ingestion of data from disparate sources, editing data, validating data, fact checking reports
  • Regulation: auditing data, verifying data, source-checking
  • Political: ensuring commitments are made and standards adhered to, raising concerns, identifying problems, issuing warnings
  • Public: oversight, providing criticism and feedback

The data may change little but the person using them, and the access they need, will.

The problem for the development team (and we use this term broadly to include everyone contributing to the site, and those marketing and promoting it), is to determine who sees what, when, and why. Nor is this a one way street; depending on their requirements, our analysts, statisticians, politicians and public may need to give up information about themselves.

Is it better to create multiple apps (data entry, analytics, dashboard) or variations on a single app? Whatever the decision (and both have pros and cons), recognition and anticipation of variable use cases is enormously helpful when it comes to writing less code and designing simple interfaces.

If teams ignore difference at the outset, contradictions accumulate and complexity increases.

How do we prevent this from happening? The first step is to consider all possible user flows and place them in groups. The number of groups is critical; the fewer the better so long as no one group becomes a bucket for miscellany. This work is not trivial. It benefits from expertise in UI and UX but ideally it should involve disparate team members, including people who understand human behaviour, data manipulation, HTML, screen readers, accessibility, performance, security, SEO, marketing, etc. It's a long list.

And this is where we may differ from others, as much work explaining, documenting and testing solutions as possible should take place in the browser.
